Summary

Building the missing CAD tool for Biotech's CAM stack.

I run genefoo.com, a supply company that sells hacker-friendly molecular biology tools, and consult on projects at the intersection of biotechnology + the public + interactive experiences.

I am a SynBioLeap 2015 Fellow studying companies and technologies that enable low-cost lab automation and quality assurance for increased efficiency and learning.

I cofounded diybio.org in 2008.

I started a public wetlab in Boston - the Boston Open Source Science Lab - in 2009.

I researched gene patents at the Berkman center at Harvard Law school from 2009 - 2010.

I made simple DNA sequencing tools at cofactorbio.com from 2011 - 2012.

I was one of the first 10 "founding father"​ http://awesomefoundation.org micro-trustees.

  • Founder

    DIYbio.org
    March 2008 – August 2009 (1 year 6 months)

    DIYbio.org is an online community for the growing biohacking movement. I run the website with a couple of friends and operate a public wetlab in the Boston area. We're using the local lab to validate some of the projects other community members are working on and to host one of the first all-amateur iGEM teams.

    We're also taking a leadership role in understanding the safety, security, and social issues surrounding distributed garage-scale biotechnology.

  • Generalist

    iGEM
    August 2006 – April 2008 (1 year 9 months)

    After participating in iGEM on the 2005 Davidson team, I knew I had to get involved in the competition and in the field of Synthetic Biology. I moved to Boston in the summer of 2006 and joined the iGEM staff in August. My main directive was to enable iGEM teams to be more successful with their projects. I would answer emails, write educational content for the website, design new websites, and do various lab work wehn the full-time tech, Meagan Lizarazo, needed an extra hand. Notably, I spent about 6 weeks in 2007 making 150 copies of the 2007 iGEM parts distribution (about 1500 standard biological parts). I used a variety of 96- and 384-well plate automation to complete that task.

  • Summer Research Fellow

    Howard Hughes Medical Institute
    May 2005 – August 2005 (4 months)

    I worked with Professor Laurie Heyer at Davidson in the Mathematics and Biology department on MAGIC Tool, an software package package for teaching and analyzing microarrays (written in Java).

  • Teaching Assistant at GCAT workshop

    Davidson College
    July 2005 – July 2005 (1 month)

    Hosted by Morehouse College in Atlanta, I Helped teach 43 undergraduate professors how to use MAGICTool at the third annual Genome Consortium for Active Teaching (GCAT) summer workshop.

  • NSF REU Summer Researcher

    Arctic Region Supercomputing Center
    May 2004 – August 2004 (4 months)

    I was awarded an REU scholarship from the NSF to spend 10 weeks doing research at the Arctic Region Supercomputing Center at the University of Fairbanks, Alaska.

    I wrote a program (in C with VTK) to model and animate a model of human lungs based on the Visible Human dataset and to drive that model with physiological data.

    I presented the work, "Virtual Lungs: foundations for a dynamically-generated, tomographically-derived real-time 3D model" at the Triangle Undergraduate Research Symposium (North Carolina State University) in Nov '04) and at the Carolinas Mathematics Undergraduate Research Conference (Davidson College) on Apr '05.
